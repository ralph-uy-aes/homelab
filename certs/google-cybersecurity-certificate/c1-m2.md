# Common Attacks and Their Effectiveness

## Phishing
Digital communications tricking users into revealing data or deploying malware.

- **Business Email Compromise (BEC)** – Impersonates a known source for financial gain.
- **Spear phishing** – Targets specific users, appearing trusted.
- **Whaling** – Spear phishing aimed at executives.
- **Vishing** – Voice communication exploitation.
- **Smishing** – SMS-based phishing.

## Malware
Software designed to harm devices or networks, often for money or intelligence.

- **Viruses** – Require user initiation (e.g., opening attachments); self-replicate within files.
- **Worms** – Self-replicate and spread across networks without user action.
- **Ransomware** – Encrypts data; demands payment for decryption.
- **Spyware** – Collects and sells personal data without consent.

## Social Engineering
Manipulation exploiting human error to gain information or access.

**Common types:**
- Social media phishing – using public profiles to tailor attacks.
- Watering hole attack – compromising websites frequented by a target group.
- USB baiting – leaving infected USB sticks for employees.
- Physical social engineering – impersonating staff/vendors for physical access.

**Why it works (principles):**
- Authority, intimidation, consensus (social proof), scarcity, familiarity, trust, urgency.

# Threat Actors and Hackers

## Threat Actor Types
- **APT (Advanced Persistent Threat)** – Highly skilled, long‑term, targets critical infrastructure or intellectual property.
- **Insider threats** – Authorized users abusing access for sabotage, espionage, or data leaks.
- **Hacktivists** – Politically motivated (protests, propaganda, social change).

## Hacker Categories
- **Authorized (ethical)** – Follow laws and ethics; conduct risk evaluations.
- **Semi‑authorized** – Researchers who find vulnerabilities but do not exploit them.
- **Unauthorized (unethical)** – Malicious actors for financial gain.

**Other profiles:**
- Beginners – hack to learn, seek revenge, or use existing tools.
- Contractors – work for pay, may be ethical or unethical.
- Vigilantes – aim to protect from unethical hackers.

*Understanding motivations helps build better defenses.*
