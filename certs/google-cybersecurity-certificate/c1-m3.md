# Controls, Frameworks, and Compliance

Security frameworks, controls, and compliance regulations work together to manage risk and ensure organizational accountability.

## Core Concepts

- **CIA Triad** – Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability. Foundational principles for establishing controls.
- **Security controls** – Safeguards that reduce specific risks.
- **Security frameworks** – Guidelines with four core components:
  1. Identify and document security goals.
  2. Set guidelines to achieve them.
  3. Implement strong security processes.
  4. Monitor and communicate results.
- **Compliance** – Adhering to internal standards and external regulations.

## Key Frameworks, Controls, and Regulations

| Framework/Regulation | Scope / Purpose |
|----------------------|----------------|
| **NIST CSF** | Voluntary U.S. framework with standards and best practices for managing cybersecurity risk. |
| **NIST RMF** | Risk management framework for federal systems. |
| **FERC-NERC** | U.S. regulation for electricity and power grid organizations; mandates CIP Reliability Standards. |
| **FedRAMP** | U.S. federal program standardizing security assessment and authorization for cloud services. |
| **CIS (Center for Internet Security)** | Nonprofit providing actionable controls to safeguard systems and guide incident response. |
| **GDPR** | E.U. regulation protecting residents' data privacy; breach notification required within 72 hours. |
| **PCI DSS** | International standard for secure handling of credit card information to reduce fraud. |
| **HIPAA** | U.S. law protecting patients' health information (PHI); includes Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification rules. |
| **HITRUST** | Framework helping institutions meet HIPAA compliance. |
| **ISO** | International standards for technology, manufacturing, and management processes. |
| **SOC 1 / SOC 2** | AICPA reports assessing financial compliance, user access policies, and data safety (confidentiality, privacy, integrity, availability, security). |

*Stay updated on evolving regulations like Gramm-Leach-Bliley and Sarbanes-Oxley.*

# Ethical Concepts in Cybersecurity

Security ethics guide unbiased, lawful decisions that protect private data and organizational assets.

## Counterattacks – Legal and Ethical Stance

- **U.S. standpoint** – Counterattacking is illegal under acts like the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (1986). Only federal/military personnel may do so. Counterattacks are seen as vigilantism, can escalate damage, and may cause international conflicts (especially with state‑sponsored actors).
- **International standpoint (ICJ)** – Counterattacks may be allowed only if they:
  - Affect only the initial attacker,
  - Are a direct request to stop,
  - Do not escalate the situation,
  - Are reversible.

In practice, organizations avoid counterattacks due to legal uncertainty and risk of worse outcomes.

## Ethical Obligations

- **Confidentiality** – Respect privacy; only authorized users access assets.
- **Privacy protection** – Safeguard PII and SPII from unauthorized use.
- **Laws** – Follow applicable regulations (e.g., HIPAA for health data). Security professionals must remain unbiased, transparent, evidence‑based, and continually invest in skill development.

# Module 3 Glossary

- **Asset** – An item of value to an organization.
- **Availability** – Data accessible to authorized users.
- **Compliance** – Adhering to internal and external standards.
- **Confidentiality** – Restricting access to authorized users only.
- **CIA triad** – Model for considering risk in systems and policies.
- **Hacktivist** – Hacker with a political goal.
- **HIPAA** – U.S. law protecting patients' health information.
- **Integrity** – Data is correct, authentic, and reliable.
- **NIST CSF** – Voluntary cybersecurity framework with standards and best practices.
- **Privacy protection** – Safeguarding personal information from unauthorized use.
- **PHI** – Past/present/future physical or mental health information of an individual.
- **Security architecture** – Design composed of tools and processes to protect against risks.
- **Security controls** – Safeguards reducing specific risks.
- **Security ethics** – Guidelines for appropriate professional decisions.
- **Security frameworks** – Guidelines for mitigating risk and threats.
- **Security governance** – Practices directing and supporting security efforts.
- **SPII** – Sensitive PII requiring stricter handling.
