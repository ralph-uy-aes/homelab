# Course 2 – Module 2: Security Frameworks and Controls

## Frameworks and Controls Relationship

- **Security frameworks** – Guidelines for building plans to mitigate risk and comply with laws (e.g., HIPAA for healthcare).
- **Security controls** – Safeguards that reduce specific risks (e.g., MFA to access medical records).

### Additional Frameworks

- **Cyber Threat Framework (CTF)** – U.S. government standard for a common language to describe and share threat intelligence.
- **ISO/IEC 27001** – International standard for information security management; provides best practices and a collection of controls.

### Control Categories

| Type | Examples |
|------|----------|
| **Physical** | Gates, guards, CCTV, access cards, locks |
| **Technical** | Firewalls, MFA, antivirus |
| **Administrative** | Separation of duties, authorization, asset classification |

*Frameworks and controls together establish security posture and enable regulatory compliance.*

---

## CIA Triad in Practice

- **Confidentiality** – Only authorized users access assets. Enforced via **principle of least privilege** (minimal access for job tasks).
- **Integrity** – Data is correct, authentic, and reliable. Achieved via **cryptography** and **encryption** to prevent tampering.
- **Availability** – Authorized users can access data when needed (e.g., remote access for employees, with appropriate restrictions).

*Balancing all three elements reduces risk and strengthens security posture.*

---

## OWASP Security Principles (Additional)

- **Establish secure defaults** – Application's default state should be secure; making it insecure requires extra effort.
- **Fail securely** – When a control fails, it defaults to the most secure option (e.g., firewall blocks all connections).
- **Don’t trust services** – Third‑party partners' systems are not implicitly trusted; verify data before sharing.
- **Avoid security by obscurity** – Security should not rely on hiding details (e.g., source code); rely on strong policies, defense in depth, and audit controls.

*These principles guide safe development and reduce risk.*

---

## Security Audits

**Definition** – Independent review of an organization's security controls, policies, and procedures against internal and external expectations.

**Goals** – Ensure IT practices meet standards; identify and correct failures; avoid penalties.

**Factors affecting audits** – Industry, size, regulations, location, compliance choices.

**Audit Checklist**

1. **Identify scope** – List assets, goals, frequency, evaluate policies.
2. **Complete risk assessment** – Evaluate risks related to budget, controls, processes, external standards.
3. **Conduct the audit** – Assess security of identified assets.
4. **Create mitigation plan** – Lower risk and potential costs/penalties.
5. **Communicate results** – Report findings and recommendations to stakeholders.

*Frameworks (NIST CSF, ISO 27000) help prepare for audits; controls (administrative, technical, physical) are reviewed during audits.*

# Module 2 Glossary

- **Asset** – Item of value to an organization.
- **Attack vectors** – Pathways attackers use to penetrate defenses.
- **Authentication** – Verifying who someone is.
- **Authorization** – Granting access to specific resources.
- **Availability** – Data accessible to authorized users.
- **Biometrics** – Unique physical characteristics for verification.
- **Confidentiality** – Only authorized users access assets.
- **CIA triad** – Model for considering risk.
- **Detect** – NIST function for identifying incidents and improving monitoring.
- **Encryption** – Converting data to encoded format.
- **Govern** – NIST function for strategy, policies, roles, and risk management.
- **Identify** – NIST function for managing cybersecurity risk.
- **Integrity** – Data is correct, authentic, reliable.
- **NIST CSF** – Voluntary framework of standards and best practices.
- **NIST SP 800-53** – Framework for U.S. federal information systems security.
- **OWASP** – Non‑profit focused on software security.
- **Protect** – NIST function for implementing policies, training, and tools.
- **Recover** – NIST function for restoring systems after incidents.
- **Respond** – NIST function for containing and analyzing incidents.
- **Risk** – Anything impacting CIA.
- **Security audit** – Review of controls, policies, procedures.
- **Security controls** – Safeguards reducing risks.
- **Security frameworks** – Guidelines for risk mitigation.
- **Security posture** – Ability to defend assets and react.
- **Threat** – Any event negatively impacting assets.
